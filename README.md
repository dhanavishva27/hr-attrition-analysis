## 📌 Project Overview

This is an **original exploratory data analysis** of the IBM HR 
Analytics Employee Attrition dataset. The goal was to identify 
key drivers of employee attrition and provide actionable 
recommendations to reduce turnover.

**Dataset:** IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition  
**Records:** 1,470 employees | 35 features  
**Tool:** Python (Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn) | Jupyter Notebook

## 💡 Key Business Insights

**1. Sales Department Crisis**
Sales department has 20.6% attrition — 2 in every 10 
employees leave. Sales Representatives specifically show 
39.76% attrition — the highest of any job role. Immediate 
retention strategies needed for this segment.

**2. Compensation is the Biggest Driver**
Employees who left earned significantly lower salaries. 
The average income of those who left is nearly half of 
those who stayed. Salary review for lower income brackets 
is the single highest-impact intervention.

**3. Young Talent Retention**
The 18-20 age group shows 25%+ attrition. However the 
21-25 group shows comparatively lower attrition — these 
are fresh, innovative employees worth investing in through 
structured mentorship and career growth programmes.

**4. Overtime Warning**
Employees working overtime show nearly 3x higher attrition 
than those who don't. Reducing mandatory overtime or 
compensating fairly could significantly improve retention.

**5. Work-Life Balance Score 4 Anomaly**
Surprisingly, employees who rated work-life balance as 4 
(highest) still showed high attrition — suggesting factors 
beyond work-life balance are driving their exit decisions. 
Further investigation recommended.

**6. Safe Hiring Zones**
26-35 and 36-45 age groups show below 10% attrition — 
the most stable hiring targets. Manufacturing Directors, 
Healthcare Representatives, and Research Directors show 
under 7% attrition — highly stable roles.
